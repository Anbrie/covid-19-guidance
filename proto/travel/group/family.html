**New**Canadian citizens, permanent residents, persons registered under the Indian Act, and protected persons will be allowed to return to Canada by right. However, they are subject to mandatory quarantine requirements and any provincial or territorial restrictions that could regulate family reunifications.

Who is considered immediate family

To be considered an immediate family member you must be the:

  • spouse or common-law partner of the person
  • dependent child of the person
  • dependent child of a dependent child of the person
  • parent or step-parent of the person
  • guardian or tutor of the person

If you do not meet this relationship criteria with a Canadian citizen or permanent resident, you will not be permitted to enter Canada for the purpose of reuniting with family.

Unite with immediate family who are Canadian citizens or permanent residents

You do not need a written authorization from IRCC confirming that you are exempt from the travel restrictions to unite with a Canadian citizen or permanent resident.

If you'll stay in Canada for at least 15 days, you need:

  • proof of relationship with an immediate family member who is a Canadian citizen or permanent resident

If you are flying to Canada as a foreign national

To board your flight to Canada, you must also:

  • follow all airline requirements and pass a health check
  • tell the airline that you’re exempt from the travel restrictions
  • make sure you have a valid travel document (visitor visa or eTA) and a passport that is valid to come to Canada

Unite with immediate family who are in Canada temporarily

Coming from the U.S. as a foreign national

To travel to Canada from the U.S. to reunite with an immediate family member who is in Canada temporarily, you must:

  • have proof of relationship with an immediate family member
  • prove that you're staying for 15 days or travelling for an non-optional purpose, such as to establish yourself in Canada and live with your family member long term
If you are flying to Canada from the U.S.

To board your flight to Canada, you must also:

  • follow all airline requirements and pass a health check
  • tell the airline that you’re exempt from the travel restrictions
  • make sure you have a valid travel document (visitor visa or eTA) and a passport that is valid to come to Canada

Coming from all other countries as a foreign national

To be eligible to travel to Canada, you must:

  • follow all airline requirements and pass a health check
  • tell the airline that you’re exempt from the travel restrictions
  • have written authorization from ircc confirming that you’re exempt from the travel restrictions
  • have a valid visitor visa (if you’re from a country whose nationals require a visa) or an eTA (if you’re from a country whose nationals are visa-exempt)
  • have a valid passport
  • show an immigration officer that you’re coming for an essential purpose, such as to establish yourself in Canada and live with your family member long term
Request an authorization: non-U.S. travellers

To board your flight to Canada from a country other than the US, you must have written authorization from IRCC to fly to Canada.

You’ll be refused an authorization if an officer finds that you’re travelling for an optional or non-essential purpose, such as for tourism, entertainment or recreation.

To request an authorization, email us at IRCC.COVID-TravelExemptions-Exemptionsdevoyage-COVID.IRCC@cic.gc.ca

When you contact us, you must provide your:

  • full name
  • date of birth
  • unique client identifier (UCI)
  • passport number
  • detailed reason for travel
  • proof of relationship with an immediate family member in Canada
  • proof of immigration status of your immediate family member in Canada (copy of their study permit or work permit)
  • proof of temporary resident visa or eTA

We’ll contact you within 5 business days of getting your email and tell you what to do next. We only respond to inquiries about reuniting with an immediate family member who is living in Canada temporarily.

Documents to prove immediate family status and your relationship

No longer JUST immediate family - could be used to prove relationship for "care" and "funerals/visits/family support"

You must have 2 types of documents:

  • one that shows your immediate family member’s status as a Canadian citizen or permanent resident or temporary resident
  • one that shows your relationship to that family member
Your immediate family member’s Canadian citizenship, permanent resident status or temporary resident status
  • a Canadian passport
  • proof of Canadian citizenship, such as a citizenship certificate, citizenship card, or provincial or territorial birth certificate
  • a Canadian permanent resident card
  • a Canadian permanent resident travel document
  • a copy of the immediate family member’s work permit, study permit, temporary resident permit, or visitor record (please note that a visitor record may not be suitable to meet an exemption in all circumstances

Note: Paper and electronic copies of the documents above are accepted. Please note that the documents above are examples only. An officer may request or accept other documents as needed.

Your relationship to that family member
  • a marriage certificate or proof of common-law status (documents showing a shared address)
  • a birth certificate
  • a Confirmation of Permanent Residence (COPR) for one of the following:
    • the family class (the COPR category under Application Details will be FC)
    • the one-year window of opportunity provision (coded OYW under Special Program)
  • other documents that show an immediate family connection, for example
    • correspondence from us showing a spousal sponsorship application in progress
    • documents that show a shared home address

**New section to review**Entry to care for a family member

Coming from the U.S. as a foreign national

You may be granted entry to care for a family member who must have your assistance. However, you must have compelling evidence to prove your entry is non-optional.

You will need to provide:

  • a written letter from a doctor proving your family member requires care
  • reasons why no other care option is possible
  • details of the type of care you will be providing
  • any additional details that would strengthen your case that this care must be provided by you, and is necessary

Coming from all other countries as a foreign national

You will have to request a family reunification certificate. In order to apply for one, you must be able to show that your family member must have your assistance, and why you are the only one who is able to provide that care.

Request a family reunification certificate

You could be granted entry to care for someone who is must have your assistance, and has no other care options.

To request an authorization, email us at IRCC.COVID-TravelExemptions-Exemptionsdevoyage-COVID.IRCC@cic.gc.ca

When you contact us, you must provide your:

  • full name
  • date of birth
  • unique client identifier (UCI)
  • passport number
  • a written letter from a doctor proving your family member requires care
  • reasons why no other care option is possible
  • details of the type of care you will be providing
  • any additional details that would strengthen your case that this care must be provided by you, and is necessary
  • proof of relationship with your family member in Canada
  • proof of your family members Citizenship, permanent resident or temporary resident status
  • proof of visa or eTA

We’ll contact you within 5 business days of getting your email and tell you what to do next.

Coming from the U.S. as a foreign national

You must have compelling evidence to prove your entry is non-optional. You should also demonstrate how you plan to meet the mandatory 14-day quarantine requirements.

Some examples of meeting quarantine:

  • you will finish quarantine before the funeral
  • you'll be sitting vigil in a private setting (such as at the person's home)
  • you have permission to quarantine with a family member

Some compelling reasons why non-optional:

  • taking care of the deceased estate or will
  • providing emotional support or care for remaining family in Canada

Additional definitions

guardian or tutor
A guardian or tutor cares for a foreign national minor child who lives apart from a parent, for example, to attend a secondary school in Canada. The guardian or tutor must be able to prove that they normally live at the same address as the minor child.
