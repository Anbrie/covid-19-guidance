Who can travel to Canada

The COVID-19 pandemic restricts travel to Canada. You can answer a few questions about your situation to find out if you may be allowed in.

Find out if you can travel to Canada

Disclaimer: In addition to the temporary entry restriction in place due to COVID-19, foreign nationals must meet the entry requirements under the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act and provide appropriate travel and immigration documentation as necessary. Final determination is made by a government representative at the port of entry based on the information presented to them at the time of entry into Canada.

See the rules this tool uses to determine if you can enter Canada

This tool has been designed to answer the majority of reasons for entry into Canada. The rules are different for Canadians and foreign nationals.

Canadian citizens, permanent residents, persons registered under the Indian Act, and protected persons

Canadians with symptoms

If you're a Canadian citizen, a permanent resident, a person registered under the Indian Act, or protected persons, and you have symptoms consistent with COVID-19, you should be able come to Canada by land or by sea. You will not be able to board a flight and enter by air if you have symptoms.

You must have a suitable place and plan to isolate for 14 days when you arrive in Canada. You must isolate upon arrival. This is mandatory.

Canadians without symptoms

If you're a Canadian citizen, a permanent resident, a person registered under the Indian Act, or protected persons, and you don't have symptoms consistent with COVID-19, you are able come to Canada.

You must have a suitable place and plan to quarantine for 14 days when you arrive in Canada, unless your reason for entry is considered quarantine exempt.

Provincial and territorial requirements

You may have to comply with additional quarantine time, make declarations of your arrival or other restrictions depending on which province or territory is your final destination.

Foreign nationals coming from the U.S. directly or indirectly

Temporary border restriction implemented on March 21, 2020 continues.

Foreigners with symptoms

If you're a foreign national and you have symptoms consistent with COVID-19, you will not be able to enter Canada.

Foreigners without symptoms

You must have a suitable place and plan to quarantine for 14 days when you arrive in Canada, unless your reason for entry is considered quarantine exempt.

No entry for optional reasons

A foreign national will be denied entry into Canada for optional reasons such as:

  • leisure, tourism, visiting friends
  • social gatherings, weddings
  • funerals, sitting vigil (unless you have related non-optional reasons for entry)
  • being a property owner in Canada

Entry for non-discretionary (not optional) reasons

Foreigners entering from the U.S may only enter to:

Reunite with immediate family in Canada

To be considered an immediate family member you must be the:

  • spouse or common-law partner of the Canadian citizen, permanent resident or temporary resident
  • dependent child of the Canadian citizen, permanent resident or temporary resident
  • parent or step-parent of the Canadian citizen, permanent resident or temporary resident
  • guardian or tutor of the Canadian citizen, permanent resident or temporary resident

If you do not meet this relationship criteria, you will not be permitted to enter Canada for the purpose of reuniting with family.

Immediate family members of Canadian citizens or landed permanent residents

As an immediate family member of a Canadian citizen or a landed permanent resident, you will likely be allowed into Canada if you will be staying for 15 days or more.

If you’re staying for less than 15 days, you must prove you are coming for a not optional reason.

Immediate family members of temporary residents

To travel to Canada from the U.S. to reunite with an immediate family member who is in Canada temporarily, you must provide evidence that your travel is for an essential purpose, such as to live with your family member.

For more information, see Foreign nationals reuniting with immediate family.

Travel for an essential reason

Essential travel

A foreign national entering from directly (or indirectly) from the U.S. will generally be allowed entry, so long as their reason is not optional.

  • performing essential work in Canada
  • moving of essential goods or people (air crew, train crew, marine crew, truck driver)
  • a U.S. student entering Canada to attend school (who must enter Canada for their studies)
  • invited by the Government of Canada
  • recieving medical care in Canada (unrelated to COVID-19)
  • a non health care professional caring for a family member (with supporting medical documentation of it's necessity)
  • refugee protection

Transiting through Canada

You must remain in the secure transit area of a Canadian airport to complete your connection.

For more information about flights, see Transiting passengers going to another country.

Alaska and the contiguous United States

You will likely be permitted to transit across Canada, but there are several requirements to follow.

For more information, see Transit between Alaska and the contiguous United States

Canadian waters

You cannot enter Canadian waters for optional reasons. You may still navigate through international or Canadian waters while in transit.

For more information, see Canadian waters

Foreign nationals who are coming directly from another country

Temporary border restriction implemented on March 21, 2020 continues.

Foreigners with symptoms

If you're a foreign national and you have symptoms consistent with COVID-19, you will not be able to enter Canada.

Foreigners without symptoms

You must have a suitable place and plan to quarantine for 14 days when you arrive in Canada, unless your reason for entry is considered quarantine exempt. If you do not have a plan, you may be denied entry into Canada.

No entry for optional reasons

A foreign national will be denied entry into Canada for any optional reason such as:

  • leisure, tourism, visiting friends
  • social gatherings, weddings
  • funerals, sitting vigil (unless you have related non-optional reasons for entry and have written exemption from IRCC)
  • being a property owner in Canada

Entry is permitted only under certain travel exemptions

Even if you believe your reason for travel is an essential visit, you may only enter Canada to:

Reunite with immediate family in Canada

To be considered an immediate family member you must be the:

  • spouse or common-law partner of the person
  • dependent child of the person
  • dependent child of a dependent child of the person
  • parent or step-parent of the person or the;
  • guardian or tutor of the person

If you do not meet this relationship criteria, you will not be permitted to enter Canada for the purpose of reuniting with family.

Immediate family members of Canadian citizens or landed permanent residents

As an immediate family member of a Canadian citizen or a landed permanent resident, you may be allowed into Canada if you will be staying for at least 15 days.

If you’re staying for less than 15 days, you must prove you are coming for an essential purpose, or another travel exempt reason.

Immediate family members of temporary residents

To travel to Canada from another country to reunite with an immediate family member who is in Canada temporarily, you must request written authorization from Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada (IRCC).

For more information, see Foreign nationals reuniting with immediate family.

Travel for a specific purpose that is permitted (travel exemption)
  • some temporary foreign workers
  • some international students
  • some approved permanent residents
  • immediate family members with written authorization from Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada (IRCC) to reunite with a temporary resident of Canada
  • members of the Canadian forces, visiting forces and their immediate family members
  • accredited diplomats and immediate family members (includes North Atlantic Treaty Organization [NATO], those under the United Nations Headquarters Agreement, other organizations)
  • air and marine crew member
  • French citizens who live in Saint-Pierre and Miquelon and have only been in Canada, the U.S. or Saint-Pierre and Miquelon during the 14 days before the day they seek to enter Canada
  • any person who does not pose a risk of significant harm to public health, as determined by Chief Public Health Officer of Canada, and who will provide an essential service while in Canada
  • any person whose presence in Canada is in the national interest, in the opinion of the Minister of Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship; Minister of Public Safety and Emergency Preparedness; or Minister of Foreign Affairs
  • any person who is coming at the invitation of the Minister of Health for the purpose of COVID-19 assistance
  • any person whose purpose is to make medical deliveries, provide medical services, or receive medical treatment

Transiting through Canada

You must remain in the secure transit area of a Canadian airport to complete your connection.

For more information about flights, see Transiting passengers going to another country.

Canadian waters

You cannot enter Canadian waters for optional reasons. You may still navigate through international and Canadian waters while in transit.

For more information, see Canadian waters

Provincial and territorial requirements

You may have to comply with additional quarantine time, make declarations of your arrival or other restrictions depending on which province or territory is your final destination.
