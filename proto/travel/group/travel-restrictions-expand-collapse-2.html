Who can travel to Canada

The COVID-19 pandemic restricts travel to Canada.

Answer a few questions about your situation to find out if you may be allowed in:

Find out if you can travel to Canada
See the rules this tool uses to determine if you can enter Canada

This tool has been designed to answer the majority of reasons for entry into Canada. The rules are different for Canadians and foreign nationals.

Canadian citizens, permanent residents, persons registered under the Indian Act, and protected persons

Canadians with symptoms

If you're a Canadian citizen, a permanent resident, a person registered under the Indian Act, or protected persons, and you have symptoms consistent with COVID-19, you should be able come to Canada by land or by sea. You will not be able to board a flight and enter by air if you have symptoms.

You must have an isolation plan that shows how you will isolate for 14 days when you arrive in Canada. You must isolate upon arrival. This is mandatory

Canadians without symptoms

If you're a Canadian citizen, a permanent resident, a person registered under the Indian Act, or protected persons, and you don't have symptoms consistent with COVID-19, you are able come to Canada.

You must have a quarantine plan that shows how you will quarantine for 14 days when you arrive in Canada, unless your reason for entry is considered quarantine exempt.

Provincial and territorial requirements

You may have to comply with additional quarantine time, make declarations of your arrival or other restrictions depending on which province or territory is your final destination.

Foreign nationals coming from the U.S. directly or by a connecting flight

These measures are in effect until at least:

  • July 21, 2020, for travellers coming from the U.S.

Foreigners with symptoms

If you're a foreign national and you have symptoms consistent with COVID-19, you will not be able to enter Canada.

Foreigners without symptoms

You must have a quarantine plan that shows how you will quarantine for 14 days when you arrive in Canada, unless your reason for entry is considered quarantine exempt. If you do not have a plan, you may be rejected entry into Canada.

No entry for optional reasons

A foreign national will be denied entry into Canada for any optional reason such as:

  • leisure, tourism, visiting friends
  • social gatherings, weddings
  • funerals, sitting vigil (unless you have related non-optional reasons for entry)
  • being a property owner in Canada

As an immediate family member of a Canadian citizen or a landed permanent resident, you may be allowed in Canada if you will be staying for at least 15 days.

If you’re staying for less than 15 days, you must you are prove coming for an essential purpose, such as to live with your family member, or another essential reason for travel.

Immediate family members of temporary residents

To travel to Canada from the U.S. to reunite with an immediate family member who is in Canada temporarily, you must provide evidence that your travel is for an essential purpose, such as to live with your family member.

For more information, go to Reuniting with immediate family.

Travel for an essential reason

Essential travel

A foreign national entering from the directly from the U.S. will generally be allowed entry, so long as their reason is not optional.

  • performing essential work in Canada
  • moving of essential goods or people (air crew, train crew, marine crew, truck driver)
  • a U.S. student entering Canada to attend school
  • invited by the Government of Canada
  • essential trans-border community travel; such as buying groceries or gas when the community access is in Canada
  • entering Canada if the entry is necessary to return to their habitual place of residence in Canada; such as returning from work as an emergency responder or doctor employed in the U.S.
  • recieving medical care in Canada (unrelated to COVID-19)
  • a non health care professional to care for a family member considered vulnerablementioned in meeting, but can't find the IRCC content wording/confirmation
  • refugee protection

Entry is permitted only under certain travel exemptions

Even if you believe your reason for travel is an essential visit, you may only enter Canada to:

Reunite with immediate family in Canada

To be considered an immediate family member you must be the:

  • spouse or common-law partner of the person
  • dependent child of the person
  • dependent child of a dependent child of the person
  • parent or step-parent of the person or the;
  • guardian or tutor of the person

If you do not meet this relationship criteria, you will not be permitted to enter Canada for the purpose of reuniting with family.

Immediate family members of Canadian citizens or landed permanent residents

As an immediate family member of a Canadian citizen or a landed permanent resident, you may be allowed in Canada if you will be staying for at least 15 days.

If you’re staying for less than 15 days, you must prove coming for an essential purpose, such as to live with your family member, or another travel exempt reason.

For more information, go to Unite with immediate family who are Canadian citizens or permanent residents.

Immediate family members of temporary residents

To travel to Canada from another country to reunite with an immediate family member who is in Canada temporarily, you must request written authorization from Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada (IRCC).

For more information, go to Unite with immediate family who are in Canada temporarily.

Travel for a specific purpose that is permitted (travel exemption)
  • temporary foreign workers
  • some international students
  • some approved permanent residents
  • immediate family members with written authorization from Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada (IRCC) to reunite with a temporary resident of Canada
  • members of the Canadian forces, visiting forces, Department of National Defence and their immediate family members
  • accredited diplomats and immediate family members (includes North Atlantic Treaty Organization [NATO], those under the United Nations Headquarters Agreement, other organizations)
  • air and marine crew member
  • French citizens who live in Saint-Pierre and Miquelon and have only been in Canada, the U.S. or Saint-Pierre and Miquelon during the 14 days before the day they seek to enter Canada
  • any person who does not pose a significant harm to public health, in the opinion of the Chief Public Health Officer of Canada, and who will provide an essential service while in Canada
  • any person whose presence in Canada is in the national interest, in the opinion of the Minister of Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship; Minister of Public Safety and Emergency Preparedness; or Minister of Foreign Affairs
  • any person who is coming at the invitation of the Minister of Health for the purpose of COVID-19 assistance
  • any person whose purpose is to make medical deliveries
