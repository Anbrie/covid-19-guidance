Canada.ca warnings and service disruptions

From: Treasury Board of Canada Secretariat

These recommendations will help bring consistency to each department's communications on service disruptions during COVID-19.

On this page

  • How to use alerts in a crisis
  • Alerts: do’s and don’ts
  • Multiple alerts: departmental service disruption page

How to use alerts in a crisis

Use alerts:

  • at impacted points of service delivery, such as service initiation pages, contact pages, etc.
  • sparingly beyond service delivery points to avoid “alert fatigue”

Alert content should:

  • explain why service is disrupted or warning is required
  • describe the impact on the user
  • be tailored to the page on which it appears
  • include a single link to where updates are available

Which alert to use

Danger alert (red)
Only if service is cancelled or if there's a risk to health or safety to Canadians in completing an activity.
Warning alert (yellow)
For delays, closures at certain locations, and other types of service disruptions.
Info alert (blue)
Indicate the length of time an application method takes, changes in wait times on a phone line, messages that there is no service disruption for a particular service if that is generating inquiries.
Success alert (green)
Use a success alert when a service disruption is resolved.

Example

Limited service availability

This service is affected by the outbreak of coronavirus (COVID-19).

Check service status updates

HTML to code an alert

Code:

<section class="alert alert-warning">
    <h3>Limited service availability</h3>
    <p>This service is affected by the outbreak of coronavirus (COVID-19).</p>
    <a href="#">Check service status updates</a></p>
</section>

Change the "alert-warning" class to "alert-info", "alert-danger" or "alert-success" for the other types of alerts.

Alerts: do’s and don’ts

Do

  • use alerts on a temporary basis - retain only for days or weeks
  • place the alert at the point of service/task
  • save alerts for significant situations that impact most users:
    • on the Canada.ca home page, only use when more than 50% of the population is affected
    • on institutional landing pages, only use when more than 40% of users are impacted

Alerts are particularly encouraged:

  • On a service initiation page
  • On a contact page
  • On a beta institutional landing page
  • On an institutional profile

Don't

Don’t use alerts as regular content - rather they are meant to draw attention to changes in regular content, in two ways:

  • a change to the content of the page on which the alert appears - in this case place the alert next to the updated content
  • a change that has been made elsewhere, such as new information being published - in this case, link to the new information from within the alert

In addition, don't:

  • Have too many alerts - limit the number of alerts on each page and the number of alerts used across pages- ideally 1, maximum 2
  • Don't use them to ‘highlight’ permanent content

Multiple alerts: departmental service disruption page

Coordinate multiple alerts about the same issue or situation with a departmental service disruptions page and link to it from all relevant alerts.

The central page will:

  • summarize affected services in plain language
  • explain the impact on the users of affected services
  • be updated as soon as new information arises

Keep in mind to:

  • point breadcrumbed to the Government's response page, if there is one
  • anchor to different headings for contextual messaging on service specific interruptions
How this helps
  • prevent scattered COVID-19 and service disruption pages
  • encourage a coordinated approach within each department and across the GC
  • reduce the updating required to maintain various alerts and updates
